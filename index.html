A Tale of Two Asterisks

Javascript today is a very quickly evolving language but this wasn't always the case. The ecmascript language has come a long way and part of that has been the need to adapt to current browser development speed. I'm going to take you through where it started and how we got to the new proposal process using the example of the recently accepted feature, the exponentiation operator. I'm calling this, a Tale of two Asterisks

It was the best of times...

The year was 1995 and Netscape was killing it.

  • November 1994 Netscape Navigator availble for download (or on floppy/CD's in stores)

A little history...

June 1997, 1st Edition
ECMAScript 1st edition officially published, but we were doing Math.pow like idiots.
The general intent is that an implementor should be able to use the same mathematical library for ECMAScript on a given hardware platform that is available to C programmers on that platform.

A little history...

June 1998, 2nd Edition

Editorial changes mostly, but devs were running in to a problem, string manipulation was very limited.

indexOf, split, substring, toLowerCase, toUpperCase, length

No replace. No slice.

Although substring handles many cases where you could use slice.

A little history...

December 1999, 3rd Edition

Regex added ("now they had two problems", pause for laughter).

Additionally, concat and replace added.

A little history...

October 2008, 4th Edition
Thats right, NINE years later. Also, never released.
December 2009, 5th Edition

10 years worth of killer features:

  • bug fixes
  • JSON!
  • strict mode
  • get/set
  • metaprogramming

Microsoft and Mozilla got in a fight about backwards compatibility so the dissenters got together and formed their own subcommittee to make their own version but then everyone made nice in july 2008 and came together to create the 5th edition.

A little history...

June 2015, 6th Edition

The spec is now called ES2015!

Adopted a new yearly model, new features added as they are approved.

Obviously this model was not sustainable, and TC39 realized this. TC39 is the committee in charge of Ecmascript, they are super creative in Europe, so it stands for Technical Committee 39. Ecmascript is actually ECMA262, because again, they are super creative in naming. All their standards get numbered like IETF RFC's, etc.

Anyway, after years of working on ES6 they decided that they need to change to a yearly / agile model. Whatever features were approved that year were the new spec.

The language needed to move at the speed of browser development.

What happened to the asterisks?


var a = 2;
a = Math.pow(a, 4);

Gross!


let a = 2;
a **= 4;

Ah, much better

What's it all mean?

The new proposal process allows the ECMAscript implentation to adapt much more quickly to the changing web landscape.
